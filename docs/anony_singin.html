Anonymous Shopping versus Personalization: A Model of Consumer Privacy Choice

1. The model

Consider a retailer selling a large number of different goods to consumers. Each consumer can only be matched with one good, deriving positive value from their matched good and zero value from the unmatched good. Specifically, a consumer's utility from the matched good is given by θv\theta vθv, where v∼U[0,1]v \sim U[0,1]v∼U[0,1] represents the horizontal match quality (how well a specific product matches the consumer's taste preferences), and θ\thetaθ captures the consumer's vertical quality preference (willingness-to-pay for quality) and is their private information. A high θ\thetaθ indicates a consumer with strong purchasing power who values quality and product fit, while a low θ\thetaθ suggests a price-sensitive consumer who views products as more substitutable. We assume θ\thetaθ follows a continuous atomless distribution with c.d.f. denoted by G(θ)G(\theta)G(θ) on the support of [θ‾,θˉ][\underline{\theta},\bar{\theta}][θ​,θˉ].

When making a purchase, consumers face two options: they can either log in with their account or shop anonymously. For anonymous purchases, the retailer can only recommend goods with limited information and we assume an anonymous consumer can find their matched good with probability μ\muμ. The retailer sets a uniform price, denoted by pAp_ApA​, for all anonymous consumers. The retailer can not set a different price for different goods because all of them are homogeneous ex ante.

If a consumer logs in, with probability λ\lambdaλ, the retailer can use big data analytics (including external purchasing history) to identify the consumer's taste. Once identified, the retailer can accurately recommend the matched good and charge a personalized price based on the consumer's θ\thetaθ. With probability 1−λ1-\lambda1−λ, the retailer cannot identify the consumer and thus treats them as an anonymous consumer.

Timing

The game proceeds as follows:

  1. The retailer sets prices:

    • A uniform price pAp_ApA​ for anonymous transactions
    • Personalized prices pI(θ)p_I(\theta)pI​(θ) for identified consumers

  2. After observing these prices, consumers choose their shopping mode (login or anonymous).

  3. Product matching and purchasing occurs:

    • For logged-in consumers:
      • With probability λ\lambdaλ: Consumer is identified, receives matched good, and faces price pI(θ)p_I(\theta)pI​(θ)
      • With probability (1−λ)(1-\lambda)(1−λ): Consumer remains unidentified, receives random good, and faces price pAp_ApA​
    • For anonymous consumers:
      • With probability μ\muμ: Consumer finds matched good
      • With probability (1−μ)(1-\mu)(1−μ): Consumer receives random good
      • All anonymous transactions are priced at pAp_ApA​
    • In all cases:
      • If matched: Consumer draws match quality v∼U[0,1]v \sim U[0,1]v∼U[0,1] and decides whether to purchase
      • If unmatched: Consumer receives zero value and does not purchase

Comparative Statics

Our numerical analysis of the comparative statics reveals how the threshold θ∗\theta^\astθ∗ responds to changes in identification probability λ\lambdaλ and matching probability μ\muμ:

  1. Effect of identification probability (λ\lambdaλ): As λ\lambdaλ increases, θ∗\theta^\astθ∗ increases. This occurs because higher λ\lambdaλ means fewer login consumers are treated as anonymous shoppers. Consequently, the retailer can raise pAp_ApA​ with less concern about losing profits from unidentified login consumers.

  2. Effect of matching probability (μ\muμ): As μ\muμ increases, θ∗\theta^\astθ∗ decreases. When anonymous matching becomes more effective, anonymous purchase gains a higher weight in the revenue of the retailer. Thus, the retailer has a lower incentive to raise pAp_ApA​.

Policy Implications

Our model provides a framework to evaluate several privacy-protection policies. First, we can analyze the impact of data collection restrictions by examining how changes in λ (the probability of successful identification) affect market outcomes. Such restrictions might include limiting the duration of data storage or requiring periodic data deletion, effectively reducing the retailer's ability to identify consumers. Second, we can evaluate a "right to be forgotten" policy by extending our model to include an additional stage where identified consumers can choose to delete their data and revert to anonymous shopping after observing their personalized price p_I(θ). This policy is particularly relevant as it has been implemented in various jurisdictions, including the EU's GDPR. Finally, we can assess the welfare implications of requiring retailers to maintain an anonymous shopping option by comparing our baseline model with a counterfactual where retailers can mandate login. This comparison is especially pertinent for essential goods and services where consumers might have limited alternatives. For each policy, we can quantify the effects on consumer surplus across different θ segments, overall social welfare, and market efficiency in terms of matching quality.
